Positionality Statement
Learning happens at the intersection of trust, curiosity, and collaboration — three conditions all in the business of making human complexity legible.

I am a first-generation Bangladeshi American educator, writer, musician, and parent and best friend to a tuxedo cat and a cane corso. I have lived in several countries and built my career across multiple institutional ecosystems in higher education. I understand knowledge as simultaneously constructed, situated, and consequential. Whose questions get asked, whose methods are trusted, and whose experiences are legible as data are not neutral decisions. I bring that awareness into every educational and informational material I design, whether it is an undergraduate level course on the Psychology of Gratitude, a graduate level workshop on Data Wrangling, a conference keynote on The Future of JDEIAB work in higher education, a university-wide training on Title IX and Safety, or a community series on Multicultural Competency.

My identity sitting at the intersection of race, immigration, gender, nationality, and discipline means I understand pedagogy is not separate from politics and social values. I teach from the conviction that curiosity is the most transferable skill a student can leave a classroom with, and that the teacher's primary responsibility is to make that curiosity feel safe enough to practice and explore. Human beings are wonderfully unreliable narrators of reality, and that is precisely why I want my students to become critical and productive consumers of evidence.

My role is not to perform expertise from the front of the room but to create conditions in which students become intellectually curious, methodologically rigorous, and increasingly independent thinkers. I want students to leave my classroom knowing less about what I think and more about how to think.

Pedagogical Frameworks

Area I
Learning Theory & Cognitive Development
Theories · How people learn and develop understanding
▼

These theories explain the mechanisms of learning: how knowledge is constructed, how thinking develops, and what instructional conditions support deeper understanding.

Constructivism

Knowledge is actively constructed through experience rather than passively received. Students build understanding by engaging with material, not by receiving it.

In My Teaching
In International Law, I introduce the concept of jurisdiction before naming its legal definition: students first articulate what it would mean for one authority to have power over another. The formal definition becomes legible once the concept is already theirs.
Subsumes
Problem-Based LearningInquiry-Based LearningExperiential Learning (Kolb)Community of Inquiry

Metacognitive Pedagogy

Students explicitly learn to monitor and regulate their own thinking. Particularly powerful in methods courses where students frequently do not know what they do not know.

In My Teaching
I build mid-assignment reflection prompts: before the conclusion, students write two sentences on where they got stuck and what they did about it. This makes the learning process visible and teachable.

Scaffolded Instruction

Based on Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development. Structured support enables students to exceed independent capability; support is gradually withdrawn as competence grows.

Vygotsky, L.S.
In My Teaching
Early assignments carry detailed rubrics and extensive written feedback. By the final project, students have the vocabulary to self-evaluate; the rubric becomes a prompt for self-assessment rather than an external standard.

Socratic Method

Learning through guided questioning and examination of assumptions rather than direct instruction. The teacher does not hold the answer in advance; the question is the engine.

In My Teaching
In Psychology of Decision-Making, when a student pushes back I slow the conversation down and ask: is this a disagreement about the evidence, the value framework, or the language we are using? That distinction usually becomes the most important lesson of the day.
Area II
Justice, Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, Access, & Belonging
Philosophies · Frameworks · Power, identity, and systemic inequity in education
▼

These frameworks examine how power, identity, and systemic inequity operate within educational structures and curricula. They are not supplementary to pedagogy; they are the reason it matters.

Culturally Responsive & Sustaining Pedagogy

Centers students' cultures and identities as educational assets. Django Paris' "sustaining" extension insists on perpetuating cultural practices rather than merely accommodating them.

Ladson-Billings, G. (1995); Paris, D. (2012)
In My Teaching
I audit every syllabus for whose research is cited, whose populations are studied, and what the material signals to students about whose knowledge counts.
Subsumes
Asset-Based PedagogyFunds of Knowledge

Critical & Anti-Racist Pedagogy

Freirean education as consciousness-raising and liberation, combined with explicit examination of how racism operates within educational structures, curriculum design, and knowledge production.

Freire, P. (1970); Kendi, I.X.; hooks, b.
In My Teaching
Students learn to see systemic inequity, name its mechanisms, and understand their own position within it. The syllabus is a political document whether or not the instructor treats it as one.
Subsumes
Liberatory Pedagogy

Feminist Pedagogy

Questions authority, values lived experience, and challenges gendered hierarchies within academic knowledge production and classroom power dynamics.

In My Teaching
Assignments are designed to accept students' own experiences as primary evidence, not merely as illustrations of theoretical concepts introduced elsewhere.

Abolitionist Teaching

Builds on critical pedagogy while centering joy and dismantling punitive educational systems. Education as a practice of freedom and community-building.

Love, B. (2019)
In My Teaching
When conflict arises in class, the response centers repair and understanding rather than punishment or exclusion. Disruption is information about what needs more teaching.
Area III
Relational & Humanistic Pedagogies
Pedagogies · Relationships, emotional safety, and whole-person learning
▼

These pedagogies center the human dimensions of teaching: the relationship between teacher and student, the emotional conditions of learning, the body, and the practice of presence.

Relational Pedagogy

Teacher-student relationships are central to learning. The quality of human connection is not peripheral to instruction; it is the condition under which instruction can succeed.

In My Teaching
I respond to every first submission with a comment that names something specific about the student's thinking, so they know I am actually reading and that the relationship is real.
Subsumes
Engaged Pedagogy (bell hooks)

Trauma-Informed Pedagogy

Designs instruction with awareness of trauma's effects on attention, memory, and engagement. Creates predictability, safety, and low-threat environments as structural features of the course.

In My Teaching
I include content notes on sensitive material, build in choice on which case studies to analyze, and ensure no single assignment is so high-stakes that one bad day destroys a grade. Predictability is a form of care.

Restorative Pedagogy

Uses restorative justice principles to address conflict and repair relationships. When harm occurs, the response centers community accountability rather than punishment or exclusion.

In My Teaching
I redirect harmful comments not by shutting down the speaker but by naming what happened and inviting the community to think together about it. The disruption becomes the lesson.

Embodied Pedagogy

Recognizes learning as embodied rather than purely cognitive. The body, sensation, and physical experience are legitimate sites of knowing, not noise to be managed.

In My Teaching
Particularly relevant in Human Sexuality and Psychology of Adjustment, where students engage with content that is also personally and physically lived. I design with that proximity in mind rather than pretending it is not there.
Area IV
Community & Experiential Engagement
Pedagogies · Learning connected to authentic social and physical contexts
▼

These pedagogies connect learning to authentic contexts outside the classroom: communities, environments, and real-world problems that give academic content consequential stakes.

Service Learning

Community engagement integrated with academic objectives and structured reflection. Distinct from volunteering: learning objectives are explicit and the reflection cycle is formally assessed.

In My Teaching
In Psychology of Adjustment, structured community partner placements allow students to work with advising or counseling units and analyze the interactions through developmental and clinical psychology frameworks.

Place-Based Education

Grounds learning in local communities, environments, and cultures. The specific context in which students live becomes a primary text rather than background.

In My Teaching
I draw on the specific institutional and community dynamics of where students are located as live case studies for theoretical frameworks in multicultural and social psychology courses.

Instructional Design & Delivery Frameworks
Structure & Delivery
The structural scaffolding that organizes the delivery of teaching
▼

These frameworks govern how courses are architected: how content is sequenced, how accessibility is built in, and how learning is structured across modes and formats.

Universal Design for Learning

Multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression. Accessibility is not retrofitted; it is the architecture of the course from the first design decision.

CAST (2018)
In My Teaching
Every module I build includes alt text, captioned video, rubrics attached before assignments open, and multiple participation modes so students who cannot contribute verbally have equally valued pathways.

Case-Based Learning

Real-world cases serve as the primary learning vehicle. Particularly effective in applied psychology, clinical, and law-adjacent courses where application to lived scenarios is necessary.

In My Teaching
Cases are composite and de-identified but drawn from real patterns. Students do not know what the right answer is because in practice, they rarely will. Uncertainty is the point.

Peer Learning / Peer Instruction

Students teach and learn from one another through structured interaction. Research shows students often learn more from peers who recently understood something than from experts who understood it long ago.

Mazur, E. (1997)
In My Teaching
Peer review cycles are structured so students respond to the quality of the argument, not just the grammar. I provide sentence starters to scaffold feedback that is useful rather than merely kind.

Multimodal Pedagogy

Learning occurs across multiple communicative modes: text, image, audio, movement, and embodied experience. No single mode is the default; meaning-making is distributed.

In My Teaching
Assignments offer pathways for students to demonstrate understanding through writing, visual analysis, oral presentation, or applied project work depending on course design and context.

Methodology

Teaching Methodology

1
Stage One
Conceptualization
▼

Before a syllabus exists, I identify what students should know, be able to do, and think differently about by the end. Outcomes drive architecture and I audit whose voices, methods, and populations are centered, and whose are absent. That audit shapes every downstream decision.

Backward designRepresentation auditOutcome mappingUDL planningCanvas architecture
2
Stage Two
Knowledge Acquisition
▼

Foundational content is sequenced intentionally so learners develop the conceptual vocabulary to engage with more complex or contested material later. In a course, this means readings chosen for both coverage and representation. In a workshop or training, this means grounding participants in shared definitions and frameworks before introducing contested or challenging content.

Sequenced readingsLow-stakes quizzesPre-class mediaLecture with pollingFlipped content delivery
3
Stage Three
Understanding & Exploration
▼

Learners are asked not merely to receive ideas but to examine them: their own assumptions, the assumptions embedded in the research or content, and the relationship between the two. Across formats, I design for an environment where confusion is productive and where changing your mind is evidence of thinking.

Socratic questioningCase analysisSmall group discussionReflective writingThink-Pair-ShareSee-Think-WonderAnonymous polling
4
Stage Four
Application
▼

Learners apply concepts to new situations: real-world scenarios, contemporary social issues, their own professional or personal contexts, or community-engaged problems. In a course, this means assignments designed so the work cannot be completed by memorization alone. In a training or workshop, this means structured activities that require participants to use the content, not just receive it.

Scenario-based projectsResearch critiquesCommunity engagementProblem-based assignmentsCase-based assessmentsPeer instruction
5
Stage Five
Review & Reflection
▼

Structured reflection points are built throughout. Detailed written feedback on every major assignment is a primary teaching tool, particularly in online and asynchronous formats. Closing reflection asks students to trace their own intellectual movement across the arc of the experience, making their learning visible to themselves as well as to me.

Annotated written feedbackRevision opportunitiesEnd-of-course reflectionMetacognitive journalingPeer review cyclesPortfolio synthesis
Evaluation Philosophy
Feedback is not a verdict on what was done. It is an invitation to keep thinking.
Because much of my teaching has been conducted online, written feedback on student work has functioned not as a supplement to my instruction but as its primary vehicle. I developed a practice of leading with what a student's work reveals about their thinking rather than with what is wrong with it. Even when an analysis is underdeveloped or contains assumptions that need examination, I frame feedback as: here is what your argument is doing, here is where it becomes complicated, here is a question that might open it further. That approach treats the student as a thinker, and gives them somewhere to go rather than simply a verdict to absorb.

Principles of Evaluation

01
Assessment measures understanding, not memorization

Assignments are designed so they cannot be completed by restating course content. Students are asked to analyze, apply, evaluate, and synthesize.

02
High standards and student support belong together

Strong expectations and genuine support are not competing priorities. A course can be demanding and still be designed for student success.

03
Rubrics are public and consistent

Students know what success looks like before they attempt to achieve it. Rubrics are attached to every assignment before it opens and do not change after submission.

04
Multimodal opportunities

Exams, written assignments, discussions, research critiques, presentations, and applied projects each reveal different dimensions of understanding.
